The Problem

A Crisis of Contradiction

India generates 68.7 million tonnes of food waste annually, while 194 million people remain food insecure. The gap between surplus and need is a logistics problem — one that AI can solve.

"India wastes 68.7 million tonnes of food annually while 194 million remain food insecure."

— FAO Food Waste Report, 2021  ·  OWID Global Food Security Index

Only ~3% of surplus food in India reaches food insecure populations
Existing redistribution is phone-based, manual, and geography-blind
Temperature, perishability, and fairness are completely ignored
⏱️

Reactive Systems

Existing redistribution relies on manual phone coordination and WhatsApp groups. By the time a match is made, perishable food has often expired. No predictive or proactive capability exists.

🗺️

Geography-Blind

No existing platform considers route distance, road conditions, or real-time travel time when matching donors to NGOs. Food travels farther than necessary, wasting time and increasing spoilage risk.

⚖️

Fairness-Unaware

Large, well-connected NGOs consistently receive the majority of donations. Smaller NGOs serving equally vulnerable populations receive nothing. Gini coefficients above 0.9 are common in ad-hoc systems.

Novel Contributions

The Intelligence Behind the System

Six novel technical contributions distinguishing Feedify from every prior food redistribution system in literature.

NC1

Temperature-Aware Perishability Index

Food decay accelerates non-linearly with ambient heat. Our PI formula integrates real-time temperature data to produce a dynamic perishability score updated hourly.

PI_final = min(1.0, PI_base + 0.15 × max(0, (T−25)/15))
NC2

Per-NGO Demand Forecasting

500 individual Random Forest models — one per NGO — predict daily demand using historical intake patterns, calendar features (festivals, weekends), and rolling window statistics.

RF_ngo_i → ŷ_demand(day) ± σ_confidence
NC3

Weather-Adjusted Expiry Risk

Open-Meteo Archive API provides real hourly temperature readings across all 14 Indian cities. This drives expiry risk scoring far beyond fixed shelf-life estimates used in prior work.

ExpRisk(t) = f(T_ambient, humidity, food_category)
NC4

PAPS Allocation Algorithm

The first allocation score in food redistribution literature that simultaneously optimizes perishability, demand fit, distance, capacity, and time urgency — while monitoring Gini fairness.

PAPS = 0.35·PI + 0.25·DS + 0.20·Dist + 0.10·Cap + 0.10·TU
NC5

Pareto Multi-Objective Optimization

Scalarization of three competing objectives — waste minimization, distributional fairness (Gini), and delivery efficiency — into a single tractable optimization problem solved in real time.

min λ₁·Waste + λ₂·Gini + λ₃·DeliveryTime
NC6

Cold-Chain-Aware Routing

Delivery route optimization respects each NGO's storage type (Refrigerated / Ambient / Dry). Food requiring cold-chain is matched only to NGOs with appropriate storage, preventing quality loss.

Route(s) ⊆ {NGOs : storage_type ∈ compatible(food)}

Ablation Study

What Makes the Model Work?

Each feature group was removed independently. RMSE increase (↑ = more error = feature was important) quantifies each group's contribution to predictive accuracy.

RMSE Increase When Feature Group Removed
Festival / Weekend Flags +15.6
Most critical feature. Indian festival calendars drive massive demand spikes (Diwali, Eid, weddings) — removing this collapses model accuracy.
Rolling 7-Day Statistics +13.7
7-day rolling mean and std capture short-term donation trends and NGO demand rhythms — second most important group.
Lag Features +1.0
Previous-day values contribute marginally; rolling stats already capture most temporal autocorrelation.
Temporal (Month / Quarter) −0.1
Redundant with rolling features; removing adds negligible error. Safe to exclude in low-memory deployments.
Ablation Insight Festival and rolling window features are the two most critical drivers of prediction accuracy — removing them increases RMSE by over 15 units (>380% relative increase from baseline RMSE of 3.92).

Delivery Routing

Delivering Efficiently

Comparison of Greedy Nearest-Neighbor routing against Two-Opt optimization under Haversine (straight-line) distance metric. Key finding: road network geometry matters.

🗺️
Greedy Nearest-Neighbor
4,574.82 km
Mean total route distance
Fast, consistent, and simple. Greedy approach achieves competitive routes due to dense city-level clustering. Runtime: O(n²). Suitable for real-time deployment.
Wins 84% of runs O(n²) Runtime
🔄
Two-Opt Optimization
5,248.01 km
Mean total route distance
Classical TSP improvement heuristic. Under Haversine straight-line distances, edge-swap improvements are limited — triangle inequality holds too tightly. Wins only 16% of instances.
16% Win Rate O(n²) per Pass
Honest Limitation & Future Work Two-Opt showed a 16% win rate under Haversine straight-line distances. Real road network integration via OSRM (OpenStreetMap Routing Machine) is strongly recommended for production deployment — actual road distances with turn penalties fundamentally change the optimization landscape and would likely reverse this result. This remains the primary open research direction.
